Social Media Algorithms and How Their Usage Influences Society

What Are Social Media Algorithms?

Social media algorithms are automated computational systems that determine the content users see on digital platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter. These algorithms are designed to personalise and optimise the user experience by analysing patterns in individual behaviour, including likes, shares, comments, clicks, and viewing time. Rather than displaying all posts in real-time chronological order, algorithms rank and filter content based on what is most likely to engage a specific user (The Impact of Social Media Algorithms: Benefits and Controversies, 2024).

At their core, these algorithms serve as gatekeepers of online information. They decide which posts are promoted to a user's feed, which ones are deprioritised, and which might not be shown at all. According to the Bipartisan Policy Center, social media algorithms work to curate the overwhelming volume of online content into a manageable stream tailored to user interests (Draper & Neschke, 2023). This customisation transforms the way individuals experience digital environments and participate in online communities, often without realising that the information presented is being selectively filtered.

The personalisation enabled by social media algorithms can be incredibly precise. Through machine learning and data analytics, platforms track user preferences and continuously update recommendations in real time. This process allows platforms to deliver content that maximises engagement, but it also raises questions about autonomy, influence, and control over information access.

How Do Social Media Algorithms Work?

The inner workings of social media algorithms vary between platforms but are generally governed by a mixture of engagement metrics, predictive modelling, and content analysis. Common techniques include engagement-based ranking, collaborative filtering, and semantic analysis. These systems are powered by machine learning models that evolve based on user interactions and platform goals.

Engagement-based ranking is one of the most widespread methods. In this approach, posts that receive more likes, comments, shares, and time spent viewing are ranked higher in other users' feeds. For example, TikTok's algorithm prioritises watch time, meaning videos that are watched to completion are more likely to be promoted to other users (The Impact of Social Media Algorithms: Benefits and Controversies, 2024). Instagram's algorithm, by contrast, considers recency, user interaction history, and content type when deciding what to display (Adisa, 2023).

Another key component is collaborative filtering. This technique compares user behaviours to those of others with similar interests and recommends content accordingly. For example, if one user enjoys a specific type of fitness video, the algorithm may suggest that content to another user with similar interaction patterns. Natural language processing (NLP) and computer vision further enhance this process by analysing captions, hashtags, and visual elements to categorise and rank content more accurately (Adisa, 2023).

However, the transparency of how these algorithms operate is often limited. Many users are unaware of the criteria used to shape their feeds, and platform companies rarely disclose detailed information about their algorithmic decision-making processes. As Science News Today explains, users have little insight into how their feeds are shaped, and companies rarely disclose how algorithmic ranking decisions are made (Muhammad Tuhin, 2025). This lack of transparency contributes to growing concerns about manipulation, bias, and the ethical use of data.

Moreover, algorithms are dynamic and frequently updated. Platform developers continuously tweak these systems in response to user feedback, competitive pressures, and business objectives. As a result, users and content creators must constantly adapt to new algorithmic patterns, often without clear guidance. This has implications not just for individual experience but for broader social dynamics, including the visibility of news, trends, and political messaging.

Why Are Social Media Algorithms Important?

The importance of social media algorithms lies in their immense influence on public discourse, consumer behaviour, mental health, and social relationships. As digital gatekeepers, algorithms do more than just filter content - they shape reality for billions of people. The way they prioritise certain topics, amplify specific voices, and suppress others has far-reaching consequences for how information is perceived and shared in modern society.

One of the most significant impacts of these algorithms is on information accessibility. While personalisation can improve relevance, it also narrows the diversity of content that users are exposed to. This phenomenon, often referred to as an “echo chamber,” occurs when algorithms reinforce existing beliefs by consistently showing users content aligned with their preferences (Muhammad Tuhin, 2025). As users become increasingly isolated within these digital bubbles, opportunities for meaningful dialogue and exposure to alternative viewpoints diminish.

Additionally, social media algorithms play a critical role in shaping public opinion and political engagement. Content that is emotionally charged or sensational tends to generate more engagement and is therefore more likely to be promoted by the algorithm. This creates a feedback loop where polarising content is amplified, contributing to societal division. While such content may boost platform engagement metrics, it may also distort users' understanding of important social and political issues (Draper & Neschke, 2023).

Another area of concern is the psychological impact of algorithm-driven content consumption. Algorithms often prioritise material that elicits strong emotional reactions - whether positive or negative - which can affect users' mood, self-esteem, and mental health. According to Stanford HAI, these algorithmic systems can amplify anxiety and depression, especially in adolescents, by promoting harmful comparison and polarising content (Qiu, 2021). By constantly surfacing idealised lifestyles or controversial topics, algorithms can distort users' perceptions of reality and affect emotional well-being.

At the same time, algorithms offer opportunities for connection and community-building. When used ethically, they can help users discover valuable content, connect with like-minded individuals, and access supportive communities. As the Bipartisan Policy Center (Draper & Neschke, 2023) notes, when used responsibly, algorithms can increase access to helpful information and connect users with communities of shared interests. Thus, the challenge lies not in rejecting algorithms altogether, but in critically evaluating their design, use, and broader societal implications.

The need to investigate and understand social media algorithms is more urgent than ever. As these systems become increasingly complex and embedded in daily life, it is essential to ensure that they function transparently, fairly, and in the public interest. Encouraging greater algorithmic literacy among users, advocating for transparent platform policies, and fostering academic and regulatory scrutiny are all necessary steps to ensure that algorithms support, rather than undermine, democratic and social well-being.
